Can You Get a Loan with a 620 Credit Score and Bad History?

  • Home
  • Blog
  • Blog Details

Let’s be brutally honest. Staring at a 620 credit score feels like being stuck in financial purgatory. You’re not in the depths of the 500s, but you’re certainly not welcome at the prime-rate party. You see the headlines: inflation is squeezing household budgets, global supply chain snarls are making everything more expensive, and the specter of a recession looms. In this turbulent economic climate, your question isn’t just personal—it’s a reflection of a widespread struggle. A 620 score, often accompanied by a few blemishes like late payments or a past collection account, sits right on that frustrating borderline. The short answer is yes, you can get a loan. But the real question is, what kind of loan, at what cost, and is it the right move for you right now?

This number, 620, is more than just a digit. It’s a story. It’s the story of an unexpected medical bill that forced a tough choice, a period of unemployment during a shaky job market, or simply the cumulative effect of life happening faster than your paycheck could keep up. In a world grappling with the aftermath of a pandemic and geopolitical instability, your credit score is a personal ledger of that global stress. So, before we dive into the mechanics of getting a loan, let’s acknowledge the reality: you are not alone, and this is not an insurmountable problem.

Understanding Your 620 Credit Score in Today's Economy

A 620 FICO Score is generally classified as "Fair" or, by some lenders, "Subprime." You’re considered a higher risk. Lenders, still skittish from economic uncertainties, look at your profile and see a higher statistical probability of default. This isn't personal; it's a cold, hard algorithm making a calculated guess.

What Your "Bad History" Really Means to Lenders

"Bad history" is a broad term. Its impact varies significantly:

  • Recent Late Payments: A payment that was 30, 60, or 90 days late in the last 12-24 months is a massive red flag. It tells lenders you are currently having trouble managing your debts.
  • Collections Accounts: These are major negatives, especially if they are recent. Paid collections are better than unpaid ones, but the stain remains.
  • Charge-Offs: This is when a lender has given up on collecting the debt and written it off as a loss. This is one of the most severe negative items.
  • Foreclosures or Repossessions: These indicate a failure to pay a major secured loan and will haunt your report for years.
  • Bankruptcy: A recent bankruptcy is a significant barrier, though not an impossible one to overcome after some time has passed.

The context matters. A single late payment from two years ago is far less damaging than a recent pattern of delinquency.

The Global Squeeze: How Macro-Economics Affect Your Micro-Loan

You can't talk about loans in 2024 without mentioning the Federal Reserve. In a battle against rampant inflation, the Fed has aggressively raised interest rates. This is the new, harsh reality. Even borrowers with excellent credit are paying more for loans. For you, with a 620 score, the effect is magnified. The "risk premium" lenders charge on top of the already-high benchmark rates can make borrowing exceptionally expensive. What might have been a 10% APR a few years ago could now be 18%, 25%, or even higher. This global monetary policy directly impacts your wallet.

The Loan Landscape: What's Actually Available with a 620 Score?

While the doors to the best loan products are closed, several others are still slightly ajar. Your mission is to find the one with the least punishing terms.

1. Personal Loans: A Cautious Path Forward

This is the most likely category for an unsecured loan. You will not be getting offers from traditional big banks. Your hunting ground will be:

  • Online Lenders: Companies like Upstart, Avant, LendingClub, and Upgrade specialize in working with borrowers who have fair credit. They use alternative data (like your education, job history) alongside your credit score to make decisions.
  • Credit Unions: Often the unsung heroes for borrowers in your situation. Credit unions are not-for-profit and may be more willing to consider your entire financial picture, not just your score. They often offer "credit builder" or small, member-purpose loans with better rates than for-profit online lenders.

The Stark Reality: Be prepared for high APRs, potentially into the double-digits. Loan amounts may be smaller, and terms shorter. You must read the fine print for any origination fees (a fee taken out of the loan proceeds) or prepayment penalties.

2. Auto Loans: The Secured Loan Advantage

This is often the most accessible loan for someone with a 620 score because the car itself serves as collateral. If you default, the lender can repossess the vehicle.

  • Dealer-Financed Loans: "Buy Here, Pay Here" lots are notorious for high rates but are often willing to lend to almost anyone. Proceed with extreme caution.
  • Special Finance Departments: Major dealerships have departments that work with lenders specializing in subprime auto loans. You will get a better car than from a BHPH lot, but the interest rate will still be high.
  • Credit Union Auto Loans: Again, your best bet. Get pre-approved at a credit union before you ever set foot on a dealership lot. This gives you a bargaining chip and protects you from being pushed into a disastrous financing deal.

3. Mortgages: The Highest Hurdle

Getting a mortgage with a 620 score is difficult, but not impossible, primarily through government-backed programs.

  • FHA Loans: This is your primary path to homeownership. The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) insures loans for borrowers with scores as low as 580 (with a 3.5% down payment). With a 620, you qualify, but you'll need a minimum 3.5% down payment. The catch? You'll pay an Upfront Mortgage Insurance Premium (MIP) and an annual MIP for the life of the loan in most cases, adding significantly to your cost.
  • VA Loans: For eligible veterans, service members, and their spouses, VA loans (guaranteed by the Department of Veterans Affairs) often have no minimum credit score set by the VA, though lenders typically impose their own, sometimes as low as 620.
  • Conventional Loans: These are generally out of reach. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac's standard minimum is 620, but in practice, lenders often require scores of 660 or higher to get approved and secure a decent rate.

4. The Danger Zone: Payday Loans and Title Loans

These are not solutions; they are debt traps. They come with astronomical interest rates (often 400% APR or more) and terms designed to keep you in a cycle of borrowing. With a 620 score, you may feel desperate, but these options will objectively make your situation worse. Avoid them at all costs.

Your Action Plan: Beyond Just Getting the Loan

Securing a loan is one thing. Using the process to rebuild your financial foundation is another.

Step 1: The Pre-Application Triage

Do not blindly apply for multiple loans. Each hard inquiry can slightly ding your score.

  1. Check Your Official Reports: Go to AnnualCreditReport.com and pull your reports from all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). Scrutinize them for errors. A collections account that isn't yours or a late payment that was reported in error could be dragging your score down. Dispute any inaccuracies immediately.
  2. Know Your DTI: Calculate your Debt-to-Income ratio (total monthly debt payments divided by your gross monthly income). Lenders prefer a DTI below 36-43%. A high DTI is often a reason for denial, even with a 620 score.
  3. Shop Smart with a Single Window: When rate shopping for auto or personal loans, do it within a focused 14-45 day period. FICO and other scoring models typically count all inquiries of the same type within this window as a single inquiry, minimizing the impact on your score.

Step 2: Weighing the Cost-Benefit Analysis

Is this loan a need or a want?

  • Good Use of a Loan: Debt consolidation of even higher-interest credit cards (if you get a lower rate and are committed to not running up cards again), a essential car repair to get to work, or a critical home repair.
  • Bad Use of a Loan: A vacation, a luxury item, or any discretionary spending. Adding high-interest debt for non-essentials is a recipe for deepening your financial hole.

Ask yourself: Is the monthly payment manageable within my budget? Can I truly afford the total cost of the loan, including all interest and fees?

Step 3: Using the Loan as a Rebuilding Tool

If you proceed, make this loan work for you.

  • Set up Autopay: The single best thing you can do for your credit score is to build a perfect, on-time payment history. This new loan is a fresh start. Do not miss a single payment.
  • Monitor Your Credit: Watch as your consistent payments slowly but surely push your score out of the 620s and into the 700s. This is the ultimate goal.
  • Create a Parallel Plan: While paying this loan, simultaneously work on other credit-building strategies. Keep credit card balances low (below 30% of your limit, ideally below 10%), and avoid applying for new credit unnecessarily.

The journey with a 620 credit score is challenging, but it's a chapter, not the whole book. By making informed, strategic decisions, you can secure the financing you need today while building a stronger, more resilient financial future for tomorrow. The path forward requires diligence, patience, and a clear-eyed view of the costs, but the destination—financial stability and freedom—is worth the effort.
